# Stop Pronouncing Third Tones Wrong: The Ultimate Guide to Chinese Tone Sandhi

Do you still say "Nǐ Hǎo" with two deep, dipping tones? Stop! It sounds unnatural to native ears. To sound like a local, you need to unlock the secrets of "Tone Sandhi"—the way tones change when they meet each other. This guide combines our two-part video series into one comprehensive lesson to help you master the most misunderstood tone in Mandarin.

## The Full Third Tone (2-1-4 Pitch)

In textbooks, the third tone is often described as a "dipping" tone. If we use numbers to represent pitch (where 5 is high and 1 is low), a full third tone follows a **2-1-4 pitch** pattern. It starts mid-low, drops to the very bottom, and then bounces back up. While this is how you say a single word in isolation, saying it fully in a sentence takes too much effort! Humans are lazy speakers, so we use shortcuts.

## Rule 1: The Double Third Tone (3 + 3 → 2 + 3)

When two third tones are next to each other, the first one "flips" into a second tone (rising tone). It’s not **Nǐ Hǎo**; it’s **Ní Hǎo**. This rule makes the language flow much better.
<br>
**Example:** Nǐ (3rd) + Hǎo (3rd) → **Ní Hǎo** (2nd + 3rd)

## Rule 2: The Half Third Tone (3 + Others → 2-1)

What happens if a third tone meets a first, second, or fourth tone? We simply drop low and stay there. We skip the "4" (the rising part) entirely. This is called the **Half Third Tone**, and its pitch is just **2-1**.
<br>
**Examples:**
- **Běijīng:** Not a full dip, just keep the "Běi" low!
- **Zǔguó:** (Motherland) Keep "Zǔ" low before the rising "guó."
- **Hǎochī:** (Delicious) Keep "Hǎo" low before the high "chī."
<br>
The rule is simple: Three plus three changes (Rule 1); three plus others drops (Rule 2).

## Advanced Logic: The Grouping Method

When you encounter three or more third tones in a row, don't panic! It is as logical as math. The secret is **Grouping**, which works just like math brackets. How you group the tones depends on the meaning of the words.

### Case A: 2 + 1 Grouping
Take **Shǒu Xiě Tǐ** (Handwriting Style). The meaning is (Handwriting) + Style.
1. Group the first two: **(Shǒu Xiě)** + Tǐ.
2. Apply Rule 1 to the group: **Shóu Xiě** + Tǐ.
3. Now you have two third tones again (Xiě + Tǐ)! Apply Rule 1 once more.
4. **Final Sound:** **Shóu Xié Tǐ** (2nd + 2nd + 3rd).

### Case B: 1 + 2 Grouping
Take **Zhǐ Lǎo Hǔ** (Paper Tiger). The meaning is Paper + (Tiger).
1. Group the last two: Zhǐ + **(Lǎo Hǔ)**.
2. Apply Rule 1 to the group: Zhǐ + **Láo Hǔ**.
3. Now you have a third tone (Zhǐ) followed by a second tone (Láo).
4. Rule 2 (Half Third Tone) kicks in for the first word.
5. **Final Sound:** **Zhǐ Láo Hǔ** (Half-3rd + 2nd + 3rd).

### Case C: 2 + 2 Grouping
Take **Bǐ Cǐ Yǒu Hǎo** (Friendly to each other).
1. Group them into pairs: **(Bǐ Cǐ)** and **(Yǒu Hǎo)**.
2. Apply Rule 1 to both pairs: **Bí Cǐ** and **Yóu Hǎo**.
3. For the middle connection (Cǐ + Yóu), Rule 2 applies because the third tone is followed by a second tone.
4. **Final Sound:** **Bícǐ Yóuhǎo**.

## Memory Trick: The "Lazy Diver"

Think of the full third tone as a **Scuba Diver**.
<br>
- **The Dip:** Usually, the diver goes down (2-1) and comes back up (4).
- **Rule 1 (The Flip):** If the diver sees another diver (another 3rd tone), he gets excited and just swims straight up (2nd tone).
- **Rule 2 (The Drop):** If the diver sees anything else, he gets lazy and just stays at the bottom of the ocean (Half-3rd).

## Practice Tips

1. **Keep it Low:** Most learners fail by trying to "hook" the third tone too much. Focus on making Rule 2 (the Half Third Tone) very low and flat.
<br>
2. **Identify the Brackets:** Before speaking a long sentence, mentally group the nouns and adjectives. This tells you where to apply the grouping rules.
<br>
3. **Listen for the Shortcuts:** Watch native content and notice how they rarely use the full 2-1-4 pitch. They are almost always using the "lazy" shortcuts explained above!

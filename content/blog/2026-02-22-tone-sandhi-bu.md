In Chinese, the word **不 (bù)** is one of the most common words you'll encounter. It means "not," "no," or "don't," and it is essential for negation. However, if you've been listening closely to native speakers, you might have noticed something strange: its tone isn't always the same!

This phenomenon is called **Tone Sandhi**—where the tone of a character changes depending on the tone of the word that follows it. Mastering the rules for **不** will make your Mandarin sound much more natural and fluid.

## The Original Tone: The 4th Tone

By default, **不** is pronounced with a **4th tone (falling tone)**: **bù**. In most cases, it stays this way. Let's look at when it remains unchanged.

### Rule 1: Before 1st, 2nd, and 3rd Tones
When **不** is followed by a syllable in the 1st, 2nd, or 3rd tone, it keeps its original **4th tone (bù)**.

*   **Before 1st Tone:** 不听 (**bù tīng**) – Not listening<br>
*   **Before 2nd Tone:** 不来 (**bù lái**) – Not coming<br>
*   **Before 3rd Tone:** 不好 (**bù hǎo**) – Not good / Bad

## The Change: When "Bù" becomes "Bú"

The most important rule to remember is when **不** is followed by another **4th tone** syllable. 

### Rule 2: Before 4th Tone
When **不** is followed by a word that also has a **4th tone**, it changes to a **2nd tone (rising tone)**: **bú**.

If you try to say two 4th tones in a row, it can sound quite harsh and choppy. Changing the first one to a 2nd tone makes the transition much smoother.

*   **Incorrect:** bù qù (不去)<br>
*   **Correct:** **bú qù** (不去) – Not going

## Quick Reference Table

| Following Tone | Rule | Example (Pinyin) | Example (English) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1st Tone (ā) | Stays 4th | bù tīng | Not listening |
| 2nd Tone (á) | Stays 4th | bù lái | Not coming |
| 3rd Tone (ǎ) | Stays 4th | bù hǎo | Not good |
| **4th Tone (à)** | **Changes to 2nd** | **bú qù** | **Not going** |

## Practice Dialogue

Try reading this short dialogue out loud to practice the transitions:

**Person A:** 你去吗？ (Nǐ qù ma?) — Are you going?<br>
**Person B:** 我不去。 (Wǒ **bú** qù.) — I am not going.<br>
**Person A:** 为什么？不好吗？ (Wèishénme? **Bù** hǎo ma?) — Why? Is it not good?<br>
**Person B:** 我不来，因为我不舒服。 (Wǒ **bù** lái, yīnwèi wǒ **bù** shūfu.) — I’m not coming because I don’t feel well.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1.  **Over-correcting:** Many learners get so used to the "bú qù" change that they start saying "bú" for everything. Remember: it *only* changes before a 4th tone.<br>
2.  **Ignoring the flow:** Tone sandhi exists to make speaking easier. If you find your throat getting tired from too many sharp falling tones, check if you missed a "bù" to "bú" change.

## The Memory Trick: "Opposites Attract"

Think of the 4th tone as a "down" arrow. Two "downs" in a row are hard to say.<br>
**Memory Phrase:** "If the next word goes **down** (4th tone), 不 goes **up** (2nd tone)!"

## Practice Tips

*   **Listen for the shift:** When watching the [YouTube video](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/1KA_3DZFS3M), pay close attention to the speaker's pitch. Notice how the voice rises on "bú qù."<br>
*   **Shadowing:** Repeat the example sentences from the video several times. Try to match the melody of the speaker exactly.<br>
*   **Record yourself:** Record yourself saying "bù hǎo" and "bú qù" and listen back to see if you can hear the distinct difference in the tone of "不."
